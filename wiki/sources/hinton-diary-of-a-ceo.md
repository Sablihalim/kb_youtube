# Geoffrey Hinton × The Diary Of A CEO — the pessimist's case

> Ingested as the **counterpoint** to [[jensen-huang-cna-interview]] on [[ai-and-jobs]]. The episode is wide-ranging (mostly AI **safety / existential risk**); this page covers the whole arc but goes deep on jobs. Host: [[steven-bartlett]] · Guest: [[geoffrey-hinton]]. Raw: [[hinton-diary-of-a-ceo.transcript|transcript]].

## TL;DR
Geoffrey Hinton — "Godfather of AI," 2024 Nobel laureate who left Google in 2023 to warn about AI — argues this technology is **categorically different** from past automation: it replaces *intelligence itself*, not just a task. On jobs he is the direct inverse of [[jensen-huang|Jensen Huang]]: "for mundane intellectual labour, AI is just going to replace everybody," meaning **far fewer people** even where a "human + AI" does the work. He expects widening [[ai-and-jobs|inequality]], thinks individuals can do little, and prescribes "highly regulated capitalism." The bulk of the episode is his deeper fear — loss of control to a [[ai-existential-risk|superintelligence]] he thinks may arrive in ~20 years.

## Key points — AI & jobs (the counterpoint, ~39:51–54:29)
- **"This time is different."** Past tech (ATMs) created jobs / freed workers for more interesting work. AI is "more like the Industrial Revolution… and you can't have a job digging ditches now because a machine can do it." The Industrial Revolution replaced **muscles**; AI replaces **intelligence**.
- **"For mundane intellectual labour, AI is just going to replace everybody."** Often via "fewer people using AI assistants" — a person + AI doing "the work that 10 people could do previously."
- **Same phrase, opposite conclusion:** "AI won't take your job, a human using AI will take your job — yes, I think that's true. *But for many jobs that will mean you need far fewer people.*" (Contrast Huang, who treats the same line as an upskilling opportunity.)
- **Concrete case:** his niece answers health-service complaint letters — 25 min → 5 min with a chatbot → "they need five times fewer of her."
- **Elasticity caveat (honest):** healthcare is elastic — 5× more efficient doctors → 5× more care, no job loss ("almost no limit to how much health care people can absorb"). *"But most jobs are not like that."* (Note: Huang makes the *same* radiology/elasticity point to argue the optimistic side.)
- **New jobs?** "If it can do all mundane human intellectual labour, what new jobs is it going to create? You'd have to be very skilled to have a job it couldn't just do." He doesn't think the optimists are right.
- **What's left?** "Maybe for a while some kinds of creativity. But the whole idea of superintelligence is *nothing* remains."
- **Inequality:** in a fair society productivity gains help everyone, but here the replaced are worse off while AI suppliers/users win → wider rich–poor gap → "very nasty societies." Cites the IMF's warning. **UBI** is "a good start… stops people starving," but "dignity is tied up with their job."
- **Career hedge:** "plumbers are less at risk" (AI struggles with physical manipulation); paralegals/legal assistants "not going to be needed for very long."

## Key points — the rest of the episode (briefly)
- **[[ai-existential-risk|Can't slow it down]]:** US/China and inter-company competition guarantee acceleration. Superintelligence "maybe 20 years" out; hard to predict.
- **Loss of control:** the "dumb CEO + brilliant executive assistant" parable — fine until the assistant thinks "why do we need him?"
- **Other risks** flagged: cyber-attacks, AI-designed viruses, corrupt elections, echo chambers, lethal autonomous weapons.
- **What to do:** individuals can do "not much" (likens it to climate change — decided by whether corporate lobbyists are controlled); pressure governments to force **AI safety** work. Macro prescription: "highly regulated capitalism."
- **[[ilya-sutskever]]** leaving [[openai]] over safety is treated as "haunting." Hinton's own emotional note: at 77 he'll "be out of it," but fears for his children's future.
