# Intelligence as a commodity

The idea that AI is turning raw cognitive ability — coding, problem-solving, even scientific discovery — into a cheap, abundant commodity, shifting the premium onto human qualities that don't commoditize. Per [[jensen-huang|Jensen Huang]] in [[jensen-huang-cna-interview]].

## The argument
- "The one thing that we value so much in my generation — knowledge, technical skills, problem-solving, the sciences, technologies, the maths — all of that is likely going to be a commodity."
- **What remains:** "ambition and character, imagination, empathy, generosity, kindness" — the soft skills "that define a person beyond their intelligence."
- Applied to leadership (see [[leadership-in-the-age-of-ai]]): the greatest asset becomes "the desire of everyone around them to see them succeed."
- Optimistic corollary: AI "closes the technology divide" — a non-programmer can now direct a computer via AI ("you're now a programmer"), broadening who can participate (links to [[ai-and-jobs]]).

## Tensions to watch
- If intelligence is truly commoditized, does that undercut the "learn AI to stay ahead" advice ([[ai-and-jobs]])? Track how later sources reconcile "skills are a commodity" with "upskill or be left behind."
