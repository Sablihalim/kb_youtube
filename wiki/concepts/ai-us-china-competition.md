# US–China AI competition

How the US and China relate in AI — rivalry, decoupling, and cooperation. Tracks positions across sources.

## Jensen Huang's position
From [[jensen-huang-cna-interview]]:
- **"China is going to be everybody's greatest rival."** Structural advantages: a large, *uniform* market (one language, like the US) vs fragmented Europe; large, well-educated population; strong STEM and world-class universities (e.g. Tsinghua); vibrant, competitive firms (Alibaba, Xiaomi, Tencent, BYD, Huawei).
- **Export controls backfired (his view):** banning Nvidia "left a vacuum" that Chinese firms — Huawei and startups — filled, giving them "record years." Domestic chips are "plenty good" in Nvidia's absence even if Nvidia's tech is better.
- **"We're not stepping back from China."** Governments naturally favor local champions, but he argues Nvidia adds value to China *and* serves US interests (Trump wants US firms to win globally; every country should maximize exports).
- **Five-layer leverage:** Nvidia competes in only **one** of the [[ai-five-layer-cake|five layers]]; its participation "supports the expansion of the other layers," and when Nvidia grows the whole supply chain (and [[tsmc|Taiwan]]) grows.
- **Two AI ecosystems = "possible but not wise."** Because AI is **dual-use** (vast good, but potential for harm), leaders should "cooperate in AI, not just compete." Compete and cooperate simultaneously — as he does with rivals today.

## Tensions to watch
- Optimistic "cooperate" framing vs. the reality of export controls and a bifurcating stack. Huang's interest (selling into both markets) shapes the view — note when other sources disagree.
